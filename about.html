1. How to use it

The tool needs to know your current stats so it can recover your true starting point, then it searches for the best way to spend your stat points toward a goal you pick.

By default it opens in Guided setup: a step-by-step flow that walks you through the steps below one at a time and, at the end, runs the simulation for you. You can leave it for the full view at any time with Exit guided mode, and re-enter it from the ✨ Guided setup button in the header. The steps are the same either way:

  1. Pick a goal. Choose a primary goal (one kind of reward, all rewards, or a target floor) and, if you want, a secondary goal with a tolerance.
  2. Set your Ascension. This decides which blocks and stats exist for you: Ascension 1 unlocks Divinity and divine blocks, Ascension 2 unlocks Corruption and tier 4 blocks. (Infernal cards are not ascension-locked.)
  3. Enter your stats, skills, and upgrades. Open the stats window in game and copy the numbers in, or import a screenshot. The values you read off the panel already include your skills, upgrades, and infernal cards, so enter them exactly as shown.
  4. Run the simulation. Press Run Simulation. It scores every build with the fast math model, then replays the top candidates to rank them.

Everything else (cards, resource values, per-point scaling, simulation sample sizes) lives under Advanced and has sensible defaults.

2. What it optimizes

You choose a goal: one kind of reward, all rewards together, reaching a target floor, or destroying the most of a specific block (a tier + rarity, e.g. Tier 3 Epic) per hour. The tool works out how to spend your Archaeology Level points across your stats to do best at that goal. It scores many builds and shows the top ones, then a separate simulation replays the best builds to check the scores.

The number it maximizes is reward per stamina spent. A dig ends when stamina runs out, so the build that earns the most from each point of stamina wins. Earning the same reward for less stamina is better, the same way 1000 coins for 200 effort beats 1000 coins for 300. Two things stretch a point of stamina further: stamina refunds, where some blocks hand a little stamina back, and auto-crosshairs, free hits that cost no stamina. Both are explained below. Attack speed does not change this number; see Two clocks.

4. Stats and upgrades

You spend stat points, drawn from your Archaeology Level and shared across all stats, on seven stats. Each has a base cap below, and the +5 Stat Pt. Caps upgrade adds 5 to every cap. Divinity needs Ascension 1, Corruption needs Ascension 2. One point gives:

Attribute modifiers

Some upgrades (the Skill Buffs) raise what a single stat point gives, added on top of the per-point values above. For example, the Strength Skill Buff adds +0.2 flat damage and +0.1% damage to each Strength point per level, so at level 5 a Strength point gives +2 flat and +1.5% damage instead of +1 and +1%. There is a Skill Buff for each main stat: Strength (flat and percent damage, and a second one for damage and crit damage), Agility (+1 max stamina and +0.02% speed-mod chance per point per level), Perception (+0.01% loot-mod chance and +1 flat armor pen), Intellect (+1% experience gain and +0.01% experience-mod chance), Divinity (+0.20 to each of its stats), and Corruption (damage and speed-mod gain). Enter your level in each and the tool raises that stat's per-point value to match. (The "Max all upgrades" button on the upgrades page fills in the maximum for every upgrade your ascension allows.)

Damage and armor-pen bonuses

Other upgrades grant flat percentages that do not depend on any stat: damage percent and armor-penetration percent. These join the percent pools described in Damage per hit and Armor. The two largest are a +2%-damage-per-level upgrade and a +10%-damage-per-level upgrade, which can total +90% damage between them.

5. Damage per hit

Damage per hit has a flat part and a percent part. Every percent source is added together first, then applied once to the flat. This is the key rule: a Strength point gives +1 flat and +1% damage, and that 1% joins the pool instead of multiplying a number that already carries other percents.

flat   = base flat + 1*STR + 2*DIV
total% = upgrade damage% + 1%*STR + 6%*COR        (+ Enrage while active)
damage per hit = flat * (1 + total%)

Worked example: a base flat of 100, with 13 Strength and a +40% damage upgrade. Strength adds 13 flat and 13%, so flat is 113 and the pool is 40% + 13% = 53%. Damage per hit is 113 * 1.53 = 172.9. Adding all percents first is why one Strength point is worth +1% of the flat, not +1% of an already-inflated total.

Crits multiply that damage and stack in a fixed order: a hit can crit, only a crit can become a super crit, and only a super crit can become an ultra crit. Each tier multiplies the one below it.

cc = base_cc + 1%*AGI + 2%*LUCK   (capped at 99.5%)
cd = base_cd + 0.045*STR
sc = base_sc + 2%*DIV
sd, uc, ud come from your stats

Crit damage is the game's 1.5x base times one plus the sum of every crit-damage bonus, so a Strength point's "+3% crit damage" is 3% of that 1.5 base, which is +0.045 to the crit-damage multiplier per point, not +0.03. The value you read off the panel already bakes the base and your upgrades into the multiplier, so the tool just adds Strength's 0.045 per point on top.

A hit has four possible results: no crit, crit only, crit and super, or crit and super and ultra. The average damage of a hit weighs each result by how often it happens:

m = (1-cc)
  + cc*(1-sc)*cd
  + cc*sc*(1-uc)*cd*sd
  + cc*sc*uc*cd*sd*ud

average hit damage = damage * m   (before armor)

Using the stats from the screenshot above (crit 66.25%, super 60.75%, ultra 22.50%, multipliers 4.21x, 3.11x, 4.84x), an ultra crit lands on 0.6625 * 0.6075 * 0.225 = 9.05% of hits and does 4.21 * 3.11 * 4.84 = 63.4 times normal damage.

6. Armor and penetration

Armor lowers every hit by a flat amount. Perception adds flat penetration, Intellect adds percent penetration that scales the flat amount, and an upgrade can add more armor-pen percent to that pool:

pen = (base_pen + 2*PER) * (1 + upgrade pen% + 3%*INT)
effective armor = max(0, block_armor - pen)
damage to the block per hit = max(0, hit_damage - effective armor)

A hit that cannot beat the armor does nothing but still costs a swing. A block that no hit can beat is unbreakable, and the tool skips it.

For example, 800 flat pen with 10 Perception and 25% Intellect gives (800 + 20) * 1.25 = 1025 pen; against a block with 1200 armor that leaves 175 effective armor, so a 600 hit deals 425 to the block.

7. Breaking a block

The tool counts whole hits. Starting from the block's full HP, it takes one hit (which lands one of the crit results, minus armor), subtracts it, and repeats until the HP is gone. Averaged over every possible run of crits and non-crits, that is the expected number of hits:

E(h) = ( 1 + sum over the hitting results of p * E(h - d) ) / (1 - p0)

Each result has a chance p and a damage d after armor, and p0 is the chance a hit is fully blocked by armor (a wasted swing that deals nothing but still counts).

Counting whole hits is what keeps this right when a hit overkills the block. A crit that deals 300 to a block with 50 HP left still counts as one hit, and the extra is dropped, the same as in game. A tempting shortcut, HP divided by average damage per hit, reads a little low because it assumes no damage is ever wasted on overkill. The tool uses that shortcut only for very tanky blocks, the ones that take more than 80 hits, where the overkill is under one hit in eighty and does not matter. Every block below that uses the exact count above.

8. Rewards and mods

Every block gives experience. Blocks above dirt also drop loot. The XP and loot figures shown in the result tables are the actual amounts earned per hour: real experience, and real loot fragments by rarity. They are never blended together for display.

The one place the two are combined is the Rewards Score, the "all rewards" goal. It is a ranking number, not a quantity you collect, so it needs experience and loot on one scale.

Putting experience and loot on one scale (for the Rewards Score)

Experience and loot fragments are different currencies, and the game never states an exchange rate between them. Setting that rate takes two decisions, and the game answers the first.

  • How rarities compare. Every block already carries an experience reward, and that number reflects how much the game values each rarity: a mythic block gives about 50 times a common's experience. The tool reuses that scaling for loot, so the worth of a mythic fragment against a common one comes from the game, not a guess.
  • Loot against experience overall. With no rate given, the only choice that favors neither side is equal weight: value a block's loot at the same experience the game paired with it.
loot value of a block = its experience   (times a loot weight, default 1)

A Tier 1 common gives 0.15 experience and a fragment, so its loot is also valued at 0.15. A mythic at 7.5 experience has loot worth 7.5. Dirt drops no loot, so it adds only experience. Each block pays its full experience to the experience total and an equal amount to the loot total for its rarity.

This treats a block's two rewards as equal partners, so a block that drops both is worth twice one that drops only experience, because you walk away with two rewards instead of one. The one judgment call, equal weight, is a single setting (Loot value vs XP, default 1): leave it at 1 for a neutral mix, raise it if loot matters more to you, lower it if less. Marking a rarity worthless sets its loot to zero. Intellect then scales the experience total and Perception scales the loot total.

This experience-scaled value feeds only the Rewards Score. The loot columns themselves show the actual fragments earned: the block's fragment drop times Fragment Gain and any loot mod, not its experience-equivalent worth. So a mythic row reading 250 means about 250 fragments per hour, not 250 mythic blocks (that count is the Blocks per hour table) and not a blended score.

Mods

When a floor loads, each block rolls its mods and keeps them, and a block can carry more than one. A mod multiplies the block's reward only when it rolls, so the average reward uses the mod's chance:

chance:  experience mod = base + 0.30%*INT + 0.20%*LUCK
         loot mod       = base + 0.30%*PER + 0.20%*LUCK
gain:    the multiplier when it rolls, raised by Corruption: gain * (1 + 1%*COR)

average block reward = base_value * gain_stat * (1 + (mod_gain - 1) * mod_chance)

For example, a block worth 1 experience with a 20% experience-mod chance and a 4x gain averages 1 * (1 + (4 - 1) * 0.20) = 1.6 experience.

Because each block fixes its mods when the floor loads, a block destroyed by a crosshair pays the same reward as one you break yourself.

Gleaming floors

A floor can be gleaming, with a set chance, which multiplies all of that floor's rewards, mods included:

reward factor = 1 + gleaming_chance * (gleaming_multiplier - 1)

For example, a 3% gleaming chance with a 3.72x multiplier lifts every floor's reward by 1 + 0.03 * (3.72 - 1) = 1.082, about 8%.

9. Stamina and run length

Your maximum stamina, after Agility and the Corruption penalty:

stamina = (base + 5*AGI) * (1 - 3%*COR)

A dig spends stamina block by block: a block costs its average hits in stamina. A stamina mod is separate from that cost. When a block rolls it, clearing the block (by you or a crosshair) refunds stamina to your pool (up to 10 per block), so refunds top up the budget and let the dig reach further. The pool never rises above your max stamina, so a refund while you are near full is partly wasted. When stamina cannot cover the next floor, the dig stops partway through it. A dig always runs as deep as stamina allows. The score is total reward divided by total stamina spent, so a build that earns the same reward for less stamina scores higher.

There is no stamina recovery downtime. The only stamina you gain back is from abilities and stamina mods during a dig; outside of those it does not regenerate. When a dig runs out, the next one starts at floor 1 with full stamina immediately. So the per-hour figures assume continuous digging, which is how the game actually plays: time is set only by your attack rate, not by waiting on a stamina bar.

10. Two clocks: ticks and real time

The tool tracks two clocks at once, and keeping them apart matters.

Tick clockReal-time clock
Your attacks, stamina spent, and damage to blocks.Crosshairs appearing, the 2 second crosshair delay, ability cooldowns.
One tick is one swing, one stamina, one hit.Plain seconds, the same for everyone.

Attack speed sets how many ticks happen per second. It does not change reward per stamina: the same build clears the same blocks for the same stamina whether it swings fast or slow. What speed changes is how your swings line up against the real-time clock. A faster dig finishes in fewer seconds, so fewer crosshairs appear and abilities come off cooldown fewer times during it. That is why attack speed is left out of the main score and only affects the crosshair and ability parts.

11. Abilities and Instacharge

Three abilities fire on their own when off cooldown:

  • Enrage: the next several swings gain damage and crit damage. Its damage percent joins the damage pool while it is active, the same way a Strength point does.
  • Flurry: gives back stamina when cast and doubles attack speed for its swings.
  • Quake: the next several swings also deal a share of their post-armor damage to every other block still alive on the floor, each splash rolling its own crit. While Enrage is active at the same time, that share rides the same damage boost.

The quick score estimates all three so the search can weigh them: Enrage's buff is spread over its uptime, Flurry's cast refund is added back per swing, and Quake's free damage to the other live blocks is credited as fewer of your own swings. Quake's credit is discounted because it fires at random times, so the number of blocks still alive when it lands varies and it rarely catches a full floor. The simulation plays each one out swing by swing.

Cooldowns run on the real-time clock, so attack speed does not change how often an ability is ready. The buffed swings happen on the tick clock, so faster speed uses up the fixed number of buffed swings sooner. Instacharge is a chance, on each use, to instantly reset the cooldown and recast on the spot, and the charges stack when it does, so a five-charge ability that recasts at once leaves ten. The simulation plays this out; the quick score uses the equivalent average cooldown, which gives the same long-run uptime:

effective cooldown = cooldown * (1 - instacharge_chance)

The simulation also starts each ability partway through its cooldown, since a player rarely begins a dig with every ability ready at once.

12. Crosshairs

A crosshair is a marker that appears on a block by itself. Tapping it lands a free hit. The tool assumes you are idle, so it only counts crosshairs that fire on their own (auto-tap); ones that wait for a manual tap are left alone.

Crosshairs appear on the real-time clock, not per swing. Each block on the floor has about a 2.175% chance every 1.005 seconds to get one, so more blocks on screen means more crosshairs. Only blocks that actually spawned on the current floor can get one: not empty spots, not blocks already destroyed, and not blocks on floors you have not reached yet. A block holds one crosshair at a time.

An auto crosshair fires one normal hit about 2 seconds after it appears, with full reward if that hit destroys the block, and a gold crosshair multiplies the hit. Once it fires, the block can get another. A manual crosshair, which you never tap, stays on its block and stops new crosshairs there until you break the block yourself.

gold chance = base + 0.50%*LUCK
a gold crosshair multiplies its hit by the gold multiplier

A crosshair hit uses the same crit rules as a normal hit. Because these hits are free, they clear blocks without spending stamina, which raises reward per stamina and helps the dig reach deeper. For the quick score, the tool estimates that boost per floor:

M = 1 + ( (N + 1) / (2 * v) ) * lambda * t * (hc / hp)

N      = blocks on the floor
v      = attacks per second
lambda = 0.02175 / 1.005   (crosshairs per block per second)
t      = auto-tap fraction (base + 2%*DIV)
hc, hp = average crosshair hit and player hit damage (hc includes gold)

For example, 15 blocks at 2 attacks/second with a 50% auto-tap fraction and crosshair hits about as hard as your own give M = 1 + (16 / 4) * 0.0216 * 0.5 * 1 = 1.04, a 4% stamina saving on that floor.

The floor's stamina cost is divided by M. The simulation does not estimate; it plays out each crosshair with its 2 second delay and counts the ones wasted on blocks that died first, so it is the more accurate of the two.

An infernal card keeps the polychrome HP and reward change and adds a unique global stat buff, scaled by your archaeology infernal multiplier. For example, the Tier 3 Common card's infernal buff is Ultra Crit Damage +8%; at a x1.6775 multiplier it shows as +13.42%.

These global buffs are already part of the stats you read off the panel. The tool walks them out to recover your true base, then re-applies them, so changing your build stays correct. A buff that scales with a stat (damage, crit damage, max stamina, armor pen, experience gain, fragment gain, loot-mod gain) is removed and re-applied as a multiplier; buffs that do not interact with any stat (most chances, super and ultra crit damage, the gold and gleaming multipliers) simply ride along in the numbers you entered. For the walk-out to be right, set the cards to match the ones you actually own. Infernal cards need Ascension 2.

14. The optimizer

The hard part is size. Level points split across seven stats, each with its own cap. At high levels that is millions of builds, too many to simulate one by one on a phone. So the tool scores builds in two passes: the fast math model picks the top candidates, then the simulation ranks them.

Scoring candidates (the math model)

The math model from the sections above gives every build a reward per hour with no random runs, so it is cheap enough to cover the whole space. When the space is small enough the tool scores every legal build. When it is too large it scans a coarse grid of spreads and hill-climbs from the best of them, moving one point between two stats until no move improves. Either way it keeps the top builds by your goal.

The model is corrected so its ranking tracks the simulation, since two effects would otherwise inflate it, both from averaging a value before a curved step:

  • Enrage. Hits to kill is HP over per-hit damage, which curves with damage, so averaging Enrage into the damage first understates the hits. The model instead blends the hits to kill at the base swing and the full Enrage swing by Enrage uptime u: hits = (1 - u)*hits(base) + u*hits(enrage).
  • Attack speed. A speed boost on a fraction of swings shortens a dig less than averaging the rate implies, because time is one over rate. The model averages the per-swing time and inverts: rate = atk / [ (1 - u_f) + u_f/(1 + flurry%) ] / [ (1 - p_s) + p_s/speed_mult ], with u_f the Flurry uptime and p_s the fraction of speed-modded blocks.

Ranking candidates (the simulation)

Run Simulation replays the top candidates (50 by default) under the game's real rules and orders them exactly. Every build is replayed on the same random rolls (common random numbers), so the order reflects real differences between builds rather than which one drew luckier crits. This is the final word.

Goals and tolerance

With a secondary goal and a tolerance, the tool keeps every build whose primary stays within the tolerance of the best primary, then ranks those by the secondary. That is how it picks, for example, the build with the most epic loot among the builds within a few percent of the best all-rewards score. When two builds are within the simulation's noise on the secondary, the higher simulated primary breaks the tie, so a build can never rank above one that beats it in both metrics.

15. The simulation

The simulation replays a build many times with random crits, mod rolls, block layouts, abilities, and crosshairs. Run Simulation replays the top candidates at high fidelity, and every build is replayed on the same random rolls (common random numbers), so the order reflects real differences between builds rather than which one drew luckier crits.

The result table shows, for each build, your primary goal metric and, when set, your secondary goal metric, then rewards per real-time hour, reward per stamina (reward per total stamina spent, so the per-swing reward), blocks per hour, XP per hour, loot per hour, and the average floor reached. A target-floor goal adds the chance of reaching the floor. It also keeps an Est. rank column showing where the math model placed each build before simulation. The table is sortable by any column and defaults to the goal ranking. When the top builds fall within the simulation's sampling noise on the secondary, the higher simulated primary breaks the tie, so a build can never rank above one that beats it in both metrics.

Because a dig restarts at floor 1 with full stamina the moment stamina runs out, there is no idle downtime, so the per-real-time-hour rate is one run's total divided by the seconds that run took. This is the one place attack speed matters: faster swings (from attack speed, Flurry, or speed mods) finish a run in fewer seconds and raise the per-hour rate, even though they do not change reward per stamina. The Throughput detail breaks the best build down into blocks per hour by tier and rarity, plus XP and loot value per hour.

16. Finding the best build: how close we get

In short: on builds small enough to check every option, the workflow returns the true best or a build inside the simulation's noise floor of it, which is a statistical tie. On a full end-game build, where checking every option is impossible, it lands within the noise floor of the best build we can find by any means. In practice the build it gives you is the best build, or one you could not tell apart from it.

Why this is hard

Your level points split across the stats, each with its own cap. The number of legal builds is the number of ways to write your level as a sum of stat values within those caps:

builds = count of  s1 + s2 + ... + sn = level,  with 0 <= si <= cap_i

At level 100 with Ascension 2 (caps 50/50/25/25/25/10/10) that is 84,447,352 builds. Far too many to score one by one on a phone.

It is harder than the count alone says, because a build's reward is not one fixed number. Crits, mod rolls, block layouts, and crosshairs are random, so the only honest score is an average over many simulated runs. Running every build enough times to trust its average would take:

time = builds * runs_per_build * seconds_per_run
     = 84,447,352 * 2500 * 0.0058 s  ~  39 years on one core

So the true best build is provable in theory but not in practice. We do not try to. We find it a smarter way, then measure how close we got.

How the search works

A few ideas do most of the work.

Hill climbing. Start from a build, then try every move that shifts one point from one stat to another. Keep the move that scores best, and repeat until no single move helps. That walks uphill to the best build in its local area:

move:  si = si - 1,  sj = sj + 1     (try every pair of stats i, j)
keep the move with the highest score; stop when none improves

One climb only reaches the top of one hill, so the search starts many climbs from spread-out builds: the math model's top picks, all points in one stat, an even split, and random splits. It keeps the best result of all of them. That is how it avoids settling on a build that is good but not the best.

Common random numbers. When comparing two builds, run both on the exact same random rolls. The shared luck cancels out, so the difference you see is the real difference between the builds, not noise. Without this, a worse build can win a comparison just by drawing a lucky legendary. This is the one change that lets the climb, and the final Simulate ranking, trust small differences.

The math model carries the goal. Because the model scores the whole space with no random runs, the candidates it hands to the simulation are already the top builds for your goal, including the build family that serves a secondary goal even when it sits far from the primary family. The simulation does not have to find them, only rank them.

Two passes: score, then rank

The math model scores builds with no random runs, so it covers the whole space cheaply and hands over a short list of top candidates. The simulation then replays that list at high fidelity (mcRunsPerBuild runs each, 1000 by default) and orders it exactly. The first pass gets you to the right neighborhood; the second picks the winner out of it.

The plateau, and why "exact match" is the wrong yardstick

Near the best, the reward surface is flat. The top builds sit within about a percent of each other, often differing only by shuffling a couple of points between two stats. Two things follow. First, asking "did it find the exact build" understates quality, because the second-best build is nearly as good as the best. Second, there is a floor on how precisely any method can rank these builds, set by the randomness in the simulation.

Noise floor. An average over random runs carries a sampling error that shrinks as you add runs:

standard error  ~  (spread of one run) / sqrt(runs)

Score the same build twice on different rolls and its average moves by about this much. Any two builds closer together than the noise floor are a statistical tie: you cannot say which is better without more runs. We report this floor next to the results, so a gap smaller than it reads as "tied," not "worse."

How accurate it is

The gap between a chosen build and the best is always:

gap% = (best value - chosen value) / best value * 100

Where the best is provable. At low levels (10 to 13) the build space is small enough to simulate every build at high fidelity, find the true best, and check what the workflow returns. The math model alone usually lands close, and the simulation pass returns the true best or a build within the noise floor of it (the standard error above), which is a statistical tie rather than a worse build.

On a full end-game build. At level 100 Ascension 2 the true best is not provable (the 39 years above), so we build a best-known reference: take the best build the workflow finds, hill-climb it at high fidelity (common random numbers) until no single move improves it, and pool it with every build the workflow tried. The workflow's pick sits within the simulation's noise floor of that reference, and the reference is itself one of the workflow's own results, so the high-fidelity climb cannot beat it by a margin you could measure.

Why this is as close as is feasible

Three limits stack up, and the workflow is already against all three:

  • The optimum is out of reach by brute force (39 years), so a scored search is the only option. It reaches a local best that a heavy high-fidelity climb cannot improve.
  • The top is a plateau, so even the exact best is barely ahead of its neighbors. The last fraction of a percent changes almost nothing in play.
  • Simulation noise sets a hard floor (about half a percent at end-game fidelity). The remaining gap is the same size as that floor, which means it is measurement, not a worse build. Shrinking it would take many times more runs, to separate builds you could not feel apart.

Taken together: the build the tool hands you is the best build, or one inside the margin where "best" stops being a real distinction.

19. Sourced, method, or estimated

Three kinds of input feed the tool. They are separated here so you can weigh each on its own.

Grounded in game data

  • Block HP, armor, experience, fragment counts, floor ranges, per-floor spawn rates, and boss floors, from the wiki.
  • Stat effects and caps, attribute-modifier and damage/armor-pen upgrades, and the Block Bonker skill (+1% damage and +1% max stamina per stage, where the stage is your highest floor cleared, capped at 100, plus +15 speed-mod gain), from the stat panel and the wiki. You never enter Block Bonker yourself: it is derived from your highest floor cleared (read from the screenshot or typed in). Its damage and stamina percents are in your shown stats, so the tool removes them to recover your true base and re-applies them in the pooled math.
  • The damage formula with all percent sources pooled before they apply, the crit, super, and ultra ladder, armor and penetration, stamina, stamina refunds, and the gleaming multiplier. These are game mechanics, with standard probability on top.
  • Ability behavior, played out in the simulation: Enrage's damage and crit-damage boost, Flurry's stamina refund and speed, Quake's area damage (which shares Enrage's boost while both are active), and Instacharge instantly recasting an ability and stacking its charges.
  • Card rarity bonuses and the per-card infernal buffs, from the Card Effects page.
  • Mods roll when a floor loads and stay on the block, so who lands the kill does not change the reward.

The tool's choices where the game gives no answer

  • One neutral bridge between experience and loot. With no rate given, the tool values a block's loot at the block's own experience (equal weight, default 1), takes the rarity scaling from the game's experience numbers, and exposes the experience-to-loot weight as one setting. Reward is scored per stamina spent, after stamina refunds.
  • Speed is left out of efficiency. Attack speed changes attacks per second, not reward per stamina, so it only enters through the crosshair and ability parts.
  • The math model finds candidates; the simulation ranks them. The math model's averages (average hits, ability uptime, the crosshair boost) are quick, so they score the whole build space and pick the top candidates. The simulation, the same exact rules you confirm with, then ranks those candidates.
  • The search is a search, not a proof. The true best is not provable on a full build (see section 16), so there is no certainty in every case. What we can show is the measured gap: the workflow lands within the simulation's noise floor of the best build we can find, a statistical tie.
  • How a floor fills with blocks. A floor has 24 slots, and each one rolls on its own at the wiki's per-slot spawn chance for each rarity, so the leftover chance leaves the slot empty. A floor can hold anywhere from no blocks up to 24. Below the ascension that unlocks the next block tier, a rarity keeps spawning at its highest unlocked tier instead of disappearing.
  • Idle play. Only auto-tap crosshairs count; manual ones are left alone.

Estimated or unconfirmed

  • Crosshair spawn rate (about 2.175% per block every 1.005 seconds) is a community measurement, not an official figure. It drives how much crosshairs are worth, so it is an editable setting. The 2 second fire delay is observed.
  • Crosshair details. The tool assumes auto crosshairs do not use up ability charges. The gold multiplier comes from your stats, and its starting value is a placeholder.
  • The crosshair boost in the quick score stacks several estimates (blocks per floor, every crosshair hit fully used, armor left out of the damage compare, and manual crosshairs not blocking blocks) on top of the estimated spawn rate. It is the least certain number that can change which build is recommended, so when the quick score and the simulation disagree about crosshairs, trust the simulation.
  • Infernal stacking and the multiplier. Each infernal buff is taken as a multiplier scaled by your archaeology infernal multiplier; whether two cards of the same buff add or compound is taken as adding. Whether a percent buff (such as armor pen) multiplies its stat rather than adding to its pool is a reading of how these combine, applied consistently.
  • The speed mod is banked, not momentary: clearing a speed-modded block grants a pool of fast attacks (the Speed Mod Gain, raised by Corruption's all-mod multiplier) that swing at the Speed Mod Atk Rate and carry across the blocks that follow. The simulation spends this pool swing by swing; the quick score approximates it as a fraction of fast swings. Because speed only shifts the real-time clock, it is left out of reward per stamina.
  • Ability cooldown start. The simulation starts each ability at a random point in its cooldown, since a player rarely begins a dig with everything ready; the quick score assumes steady-state uptime. Over a full dig this barely changes the result.
  • The Ability Fairy (an Ascension 2 event that randomly refreshes all abilities) is not modelled in either the search or the simulation, so real runs get slightly more ability uptime than shown.
